## Complete Architecture Explanation

### THE BIG PICTURE — How It All Works Together

Think of the algorithm like a **smart DJ at a party**. The DJ has 3 jobs:
1. **Know the crowd** (User Profile) — What music do they like? Are they tired?
2. **Know the songs** (Content Understanding) — Is this song hype or chill? Funny or emotional?
3. **Read the room right now** (Session State) — Are people dancing or sitting down?

The DJ picks songs based on all 3. Our algorithm does the same with videos.

---

### LAYER 1: EVENT COLLECTION (The Raw Data)

**What it is:** Every time a user does ANYTHING in the app, we record it.

**Events we track:**
| Event | What it means | Why it matters |
|-------|--------------|----------------|
| `view` + watch duration | User watched X seconds of Y total | **#1 most important signal**. A user who watches 95% of a video loves it, even without a like |
| `like` | User tapped heart | Explicit signal but less honest — people like to be polite |
| `skip` | User swiped away quickly | **Strongest negative signal** — they actively rejected it |
| `share` | User sent to friend | **Strongest positive signal** — they're putting their reputation on it |
| `rewatch` | User went back to watch again | Means the content deeply resonated |
| `save` | User bookmarked it | They want to come back later = strong interest |
| `comment` | User wrote something | Engagement + emotional reaction |
| `not_interested` | User explicitly said "don't show me this" | Hardest negative — affects category permanently |

**Why watch duration beats likes:**
TikTok's biggest discovery was this: if you ask people what they like, they lie. They say "educational content" but watch cat videos for 2 hours. Watch time doesn't lie. A user might not "like" a motivation video, but if they watch it 3 times, we know.

**How it works technically:**
- Flutter client collects events and **batches them** (10 events or every 5 seconds)
- Batch sending reduces network calls — important on mobile
- Events go to `POST /api/v1/events/batch`
- Server stores in PostgreSQL (permanent) + updates Redis (real-time)
- Queue has a **200 event cap** — if network fails, oldest events drop instead of eating phone memory

**Contribution: 100%** — Without events, the entire algorithm has nothing to work with. Everything else is built on this.

---

### LAYER 2: SESSION STATE (Real-Time Psychology — Redis)

**What it is:** A snapshot of what's happening RIGHT NOW in the user's scrolling session. Stored in Redis (fast in-memory database) because it changes with every single swipe.

**The key concepts:**

#### Dopamine Budget (Starts at 1.0, depletes to 0)
Think of it like a **battery**. When you open the app, your attention battery is full (1.0). Every video you watch drains it by 3% (0.03). After 30 videos, battery = 0.1 — you're fatigued.

**Why it matters:** If we serve a user at 10% dopamine a high-energy dance battle, they'll skip it and leave. Instead, we serve calmer content or let them go. This is exactly what Netflix does — they don't show you a thriller at 1am when you've been watching for 3 hours.

**Small recharges:** Positive actions (like, share, rewatch) give tiny recharges (+2% to +5%). If the user is still engaging, they're not really tired.

#### Skip Streak (Consecutive skips)
- If a user skips 3 in a row, they're drifting
- 5 in a row = actively resisting
- 8 in a row = about to close the app

**Why streak > overall rate:** A user who skips 5 out of 20 videos is fine. A user who skips 5 IN A ROW is annoyed RIGHT NOW. The streak tells us "this exact moment is wrong."

#### Resistance Detection (4 Levels)
| Level | Name | Trigger | What we do |
|-------|------|---------|-----------|
| 0 | Engaged | <30% skips | Keep doing what we're doing |
| 1 | Drifting | 30%+ skips | Add more variety to the feed |
| 2 | Resisting | 50%+ skips or 5 consecutive | **Switch strategy entirely** |
| 3 | Leaving | 70%+ skips or 8 consecutive | Emergency mode — show only their favorites |

#### Strategy Switching
When resistance hits level 2+, we don't just tweak — we change the entire approach:
- **"social"** — Only show friends' content (emotional safety)
- **"trending"** — Only show proven popular content (can't be bad if millions liked it)
- **"discovery"** — Show random new stuff (break the pattern completely)
- **"emergency"** — Best-of-the-best from their history (last resort)

**Why this matters:** YouTube discovered that when recommendations aren't working, serving MORE of the same scored content makes it worse. You need to fundamentally change what you're showing.

#### Categories Seen / Creators Seen
Tracks how many of each category and creator the user has seen in this session. After 3+ of the same category, we apply a **fatigue penalty** that increases progressively (capped at -0.4). This prevents the "I said I like comedy but now everything is comedy" problem.

**Contribution: ~25%** of the algorithm's "feel." Without session state, the feed feels static and robotic. With it, the feed feels alive and responsive.

### LAYER 3: USER PROFILE (Long-Term Personality — PostgreSQL)

**What it is:** A psychological portrait of the user, built from their entire history. Updated when stale (>60 minutes old or new session).

**The 5 Psychological Dimensions:**

#### 1. CategoryAffinity (map: "comedy" → 0.8, "sports" → 0.3)
**What:** Which topics does this user consistently engage with?
**How computed:** Every event is weighted:
- Share = +3 (strongest positive — they're putting their name on it)
- Rewatch = +2 (deeply resonated)
- Save = +1.5 (want to come back)
- Like/Comment = +1 (positive signal)
- View with 70%+ completion = +0.5 (passive but real interest)
- Skip = -0.5 (mild negative)
- Not interested = -2 (hard negative)

Then normalized to 0-1 scale. The category with the highest number gets 1.0, others proportional.

**Contribution: 15% weight** in the scoring formula (via `wRelevance`).

#### 2. EnergyPreference (0.0 = chill, 1.0 = intense)
**What:** Does this user prefer high-energy battles or calm, chill content?
**How computed:** Look at the energy level of content they actually completed (>70% watched). If they mostly finish high-energy challenges → energyPreference is high.

**Contribution: 20% weight** (via `wEnergyFit`). This is unique to us — no other platform does this explicitly.

#### 3. SocialDrive (0.0 = solo explorer, 1.0 = social butterfly)
**What:** Does the user prefer content from people they follow, or discover new creators?
**How computed:** (engagement on followed creators' content) / (total engagement). High ratio = social person.

**Contribution: 25% weight** (via `wSocial`). At small scale, social signals are more reliable than algorithmic guesses.

#### 4. NoveltyTolerance (0.0 = loyalist, 1.0 = explorer)
**What:** Does the user engage with many categories or stick to 2-3 favorites?
**How computed:** Count distinct categories they've engaged with. 1-3 = low novelty, 8+ = high.

**Contribution:** Scales the novelty bonus. A loyalist user who gets served random new categories will skip. An explorer will love it.

#### 5. EgoSensitivity (0.0 = unbothered, 1.0 = highly reactive)
**What:** How much do wins/losses in battles affect their engagement?
**How computed:** Count recent wins + losses from challenge votes. More battles = higher ego investment.

**Contribution:** Triggers the ego boost system. When a high-ego user just lost, we serve content that makes them feel good (categories they're good at, creators from lower leagues).

#### Context-Aware Fields (NEW — the advanced stuff):

**CategoryByHour** (hour → top category)
- At 8am the user likes "motivation", at 10pm they like "comedy"
- Computed from: what categories they completed >50% at each hour
- **Contribution:** +0.12 bonus when content matches hourly preference

**EnergyByHour** (hour → energy level)
- Morning = prefers chill (0.3), evening = prefers hype (0.8)
- Blended 70/30 with base energy preference
- **Contribution:** Adjusts energy fit calculation per hour

**EmotionPreference** (emotion → score)
- User prefers "happy" (0.9) and "inspiring" (0.7), dislikes "scary" (0.1)
- Computed same way as category affinity but for emotion tags
- **Contribution:** +0.10 bonus for matching emotions

**CategoryByEgo** (winning/losing → {category: score})
- After winning, this user watches "comedy" and "prank"
- After losing, they watch "motivation" and "sports"
- **Contribution:** +0.08 bonus to match post-win/loss preferences

**Total profile contribution: ~35-40%** of final score.

### LAYER 4: CONTENT UNDERSTANDING (What IS this video?)

**The problem:** You can't match users to content if you don't understand the content.

**Two approaches we use:**

#### A. Creator-Declared (Primary — When Uploading)
When a creator uploads a challenge/post, they pick:

**Category** (1 of 18):
```
comedy, motivation, sports, dance, music, gaming, art, education,
story, fashion, food, horror, emotional, lifestyle, tech, prank, news, other
```
Each has an icon in the app. Creator picks the best fit.

**Emotion Tags** (multi-select, up to 10):
```
happy, sad, intense, chill, inspiring, scary, funny, serious, aggressive, romantic
```
Multiple can be selected. A comedy video could be "happy" + "funny". A battle could be "intense" + "aggressive".

**Energy Level** (1 of 3):
```
low (Chill) — calm, relaxing, easy content
medium (Medium) — normal energy
high (Hype) — exciting, competitive, intense
```

**How it looks in the app:** The create challenge page has a 3-step flow:
1. Video upload
2. Details (opponent, prefix, subject)
3. **Category & Mood** — category grid, emotion chips, energy slider

#### B. Heuristic Inference (Fallback — When Creator Didn't Tag)
For old content or content without tags, we guess from text:
```
"dance" keywords: dance, choreography, ballet, freestyle
"sports" keywords: basketball, football, soccer, workout, gym
"comedy" keywords: funny, comedy, joke, roast, prank
... (18 categories of keywords)
```

This is ~70% accurate. But it's self-correcting: if we guess wrong, user skips will fix the algorithm's understanding over time.

#### ContentScore (How ALL users see this content)
Separate from any single user's preference. Computed from aggregate behavior:

| Metric | What it means |
|--------|--------------|
| QualityScore | `completion*0.3 + likeRate*0.2 + shareRate*0.3 + rewatchRate*0.2` penalized by skip rate |
| TrendingScore | Engagements in last 2 hours (20+ = max trending) |
| EnergyLevel | Declared or inferred (low=0.25, medium=0.55, high=0.85) |
| SkipRate | What % of viewers skip this content |
| RewatchRate | What % of viewers watch it again |

**Contribution: 10% (quality) + 15% (trending bonus) + 20% (energy fit via matching)**

### LAYER 5: THE SCORING ENGINE (Where It All Comes Together)

Every candidate video gets a score. The formula:

```
BASE SCORE = social(25%) + freshness(20%) + energyFit(20%) + relevance(15%) + quality(10%) + novelty(10%)
```

Plus conditional bonuses/penalties:

| Bonus/Penalty | Max Effect | When |
|--------------|-----------|------|
| Ego Boost | +0.25 | User just lost, high ego sensitivity |
| Unseen Bonus | +0.15 | User never interacted with this content |
| Cold Content Bonus | +0.20 | New content with <5 views (exploration) |
| Trending Bonus | +0.15 | High engagement in last 2 hours |
| Hour Category Match | +0.12 | Content matches user's hourly preference |
| Emotion Match | +0.10 | Content emotions match user's emotion preference |
| Ego Context Bonus | +0.08 | Content category matches post-win/loss preference |
| Fatigue Penalty | -0.40 max | 3+ of same category in session |
| Dopamine Penalty | -0.20 max | High-energy content when user is fatigued |

**How each base factor works:**

**Social (25%):**
- Direct follow = 0.7
- Friend-of-friend = 0.3
- Preferred creator bonus = +0.2
- Why highest: At small scale, who you know > what algorithm guesses

**Freshness (20%):**
- Exponential decay with 18-hour half-life
- After 18h, content has 50% freshness. After 36h, 25%.
- Why 18h not 12: Challenges take longer to discover/respond to vs memes

**Energy Fit (20%):**
- Perfect match (user wants hype, content is hype) = 1.0
- Complete mismatch = 0.0
- Adjusted by dopamine budget: fatigued user's effective energy drops

**Relevance (15%):**
- Checks user's CategoryAffinity for this content's category
- Avoided categories get -0.3 penalty
- Lower weight than you'd think — prevents filter bubbles

**Quality (10%):**
- Creator's quality score from engagement metrics
- League bonus: Diamond +15%, Platinum +10%, Gold +5%

**Novelty (10%):**
- New category = +0.6, new creator = +0.4
- Multiplied by user's NoveltyTolerance
- Forces exploration — prevents "only comedy forever" trap

### LAYER 6: FEED COMPOSITION (Slot-Based Mixing)

**Why not just sort by score?**

If you sort purely by score, you get: comedy, comedy, comedy, comedy, comedy. Even if they're all great, it's boring. Real feeds need RHYTHM.

**The slot pattern** (repeating every 7 items):

```
Position 1: HOOK       — Best scored content. Start strong, hook them.
Position 2: SOCIAL     — Content from friends. Emotional anchor.
Position 3: DISCOVERY  — New category/creator. Expand their taste.
Position 4: TRENDING   — Popular right now. Social proof.
Position 5: CHALLENGE  — Battle content (our unique content type).
Position 6: COOLDOWN   — Low energy content. Palette cleanser.
Position 7: EGO BOOST  — Content that validates them. Feel-good ending.
```

**Why this order:**
- Start with a hook so they stay
- Social early because trust keeps them scrolling
- Discovery in the middle because they're engaged enough to try something new
- Cooldown near the end prevents burnout
- Ego boost at the end leaves them feeling good → they come back

**Diversity enforcement:** After composition, we also check that no 3 consecutive items are the same type (challenge/post).

**Contribution:** This is what makes the feed feel "designed" vs "random sorted list." It's the difference between a playlist and a shuffled song list.

### LAYER 7: COLD START (New Users & New Content)

**The problem:** New users have no events. New content has no engagement data. The algorithm literally can't work without data.

#### Cold Start User (< 15 events)
**Solution:** Skip the algorithm entirely. Show a mix of:
- Popular challenges (sorted by views + likes, last 14 days)
- Popular posts (same sorting)
- 50/50 split between challenges and posts

**Why:** Until we know who they are, show the safest content — stuff that's already proven popular. After 15 events, we have enough signal to start personalizing.

#### Cold Start Content (< 5 views)
**Solution:** Give new content a visibility boost:
```
coldContentBonus = 0.2 * (1 - views/5)
```
A video with 0 views gets +0.2 bonus. At 5 views, bonus = 0.

**Why:** If new content never gets shown, it can never get engagement, so it can never get shown (chicken-and-egg). The boost gives every piece of content a fair trial in front of ~50 users. If they engage, it rises naturally. If they skip, it drops.

### WHAT WE DID NOT ADD (and why)

#### 1. Machine Learning / Neural Embeddings
**What it is:** Instead of explicit dimensions (comedy=0.8, sports=0.3), use a 128-dimensional vector trained by a neural network.
**Why not added:** Needs thousands of users and millions of events to train. At our scale, heuristic rules are equally good and 100% interpretable. We can debug "why was this shown?" — with ML, you can't.
**When to add:** When you hit 10,000+ daily active users and the heuristic starts feeling stale.

#### 2. Collaborative Filtering ("Users like you also liked...")
**What it is:** Find users with similar behavior, recommend what they liked.
**Why not added:** Needs a critical mass of users. With 100 users, there aren't enough "similar users" to find meaningful patterns. Also creates filter bubbles faster than any other approach.
**When to add:** At 5,000+ users. Use a hybrid: our scoring engine + collaborative filtering as a bonus signal.

#### 3. Content-Based Visual/Audio Analysis
**What it is:** Use computer vision to detect what's IN the video (person dancing, sports field, food). Use audio analysis to detect music genre, speech tone.
**Why not added:** Requires GPU inference infrastructure, adds latency, expensive. Creator-declared categories are free and 90% accurate for our use case.
**When to add:** When you want to auto-categorize content without relying on creators. Google's Video Intelligence API or open-source models like CLIP can do this.

#### 4. Real-Time A/B Testing Framework
**What it is:** Serve different algorithm versions to different user groups and measure which performs better.
**Why not added:** Need significant traffic volume to get statistically meaningful results. With 100 users, A/B tests take months to reach confidence.
**When to add:** At 1,000+ daily active users. Critical for tuning the weight constants (is social really 25%? Maybe 30% is better).

#### 5. Scroll Speed / Pause Detection
**What it is:** Track how fast the user scrolls past content, whether they paused on a thumbnail, whether they hovered on a video.
**Why not added:** Requires native platform hooks in Flutter (gesture detection at scroll level). Complex to implement correctly without making the app feel laggy.
**When to add:** Next major phase. TikTok uses this heavily — a pause of 500ms on a thumbnail is a strong interest signal even without a tap.

#### 6. Time-of-Day Content Scheduling
**What it is:** Content creators schedule posts for optimal times based on their audience's active hours.
**Why not added:** Requires creator analytics dashboard and audience insights feature. The algorithm already adjusts what it shows based on time, but creators can't yet time their uploads.
**When to add:** When you build the creator dashboard.

#### 7. Social Graph Signals (Mutual friends, community detection)
**What it is:** If 5 of your friends liked something, it's more likely relevant than if 1 stranger liked it.
**Why not added:** We use basic follow/friend-of-friend. Full social graph analysis (community detection, influence scoring) is complex.
**When to add:** When social features grow. Add "friends are watching this" badges and cluster users into communities.

#### 8. Negative Feedback Loop Protection
**What it is:** Detect when the algorithm is pushing a user into a depression spiral (serving increasingly sad content because they keep watching sad content).
**Why not added:** Requires content sentiment classification and user wellbeing tracking. Complex ethical territory.
**When to add:** Important for responsible AI. Should be added before scaling to thousands of users. Use the emotion tags: if a user consumes 10+ "sad" videos in a session, inject something "inspiring" or "happy."

#### 9. Creator-Side Algorithm (Recommendations FOR creators)
**What it is:** Tell creators: "Your audience likes comedy + inspiring. Videos posted at 7pm get 3x more engagement."
**Why not added:** Needs creator analytics infrastructure.
**When to add:** When building creator tools. This data already exists in user profiles — just needs a dashboard.

#### 10. Multi-Armed Bandit for Exploration
**What it is:** Instead of fixed exploration ratio (10% novelty weight), dynamically adjust how much new content to show based on whether exploration is working.
**Why not added:** Our current approach (fixed novelty weight * user tolerance) is simpler and works at small scale.
**When to add:** When you want to optimize the explore/exploit tradeoff mathematically. Thompson Sampling or UCB algorithms.
